School Funding & Poverty Analysis Dashboard

Exploring the relationship between educational funding, poverty rates, and resource allocation across U.S. school districts

Project Overview

This interactive visualization examines the complex relationship between school funding, poverty rates, and educational equity across the United States. Using comprehensive data from the Urban Institute and U.S. Census Bureau, the dashboard analyzes how financial resources are distributed among school districts and their correlation with student poverty levels.

The analysis introduces the Poverty Sensitivity Index (PSI) - a novel metric that measures how well school funding responds to student poverty levels. PSI is calculated as: Revenue per Student รท Poverty Rate. This means a district with $20,000 per student and a 5% poverty rate (0.05) would have a PSI of 400,000.

How to interpret PSI: Higher PSI values indicate districts that receive more funding per student relative to their poverty rates, suggesting better resource allocation for high-need populations. The PSI represents the amount of funding (in dollars) allocated per student for each percentage point of poverty. For example, a PSI of 400,000 means the district allocates $400,000 per student per 1% poverty rate, while a PSI of 200,000 would indicate half that funding intensity relative to poverty levels.

Key Insights & Methodology

Poverty Sensitivity Index (PSI)

PSI = (Revenue per Student) / (Poverty Rate). This metric represents dollars allocated per student for each percentage point of poverty. Higher values indicate better poverty-adjusted funding, helping identify districts that effectively allocate resources to high-need populations.

Geographic Patterns

The analysis reveals significant regional variations in funding equity, with some states showing consistently higher PSI values across districts, suggesting more equitable state-level funding formulas.

Data Integration

This project combines financial data from the Urban Institute's Education Finance Database with demographic data from the U.S. Census Bureau, providing a comprehensive view of educational equity.

Policy Implications

The findings highlight districts and states that may benefit from funding formula adjustments to better serve high-poverty student populations and improve educational equity.
